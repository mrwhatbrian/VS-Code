The Earth's Little Engineers

Introduction

Insects are the largest group in the animal kingdom. Some people don’t like insects because they see the harm they can do:

Some insects spread disease or damage plants and structures.

But insects can also be very helpful:

Pollinating our crops

Decomposing organic matter

Helping scientists find cures for diseases

Giving clues to detectives solving crimes

Insect Anatomy

Most insects have similar anatomy (how their body is made). You know you are looking at an insect when:

Body Regions

Head – eyes, mouth parts, antennae

Thorax – wings, legs, and muscles that control movement

Abdomen – contains their vital organs

Features

Insects have up to 3 pairs of legs, antennae, an exoskeleton, and external mouth parts.

Antennae – Used to hear sounds and feel vibrations

Eyes

Insects can have 2 types of eyes: compound or simple.

Compound eyes – usually large with many lenses, giving the insect a complex image of its surroundings (a fly has compound eyes)

Simple eyes – contain only one lens

Some insects have both types of eyes!

Mouthparts

Insects have mouthparts designed to help them feed on different things – long hollow tongues for butterflies to suck nectar, or chewing mouthparts like caterpillars for eating leaves and other plants.

Some insects bite other things too – mosquitoes bite animals and humans and sip their blood.

The Insect Lifecycle

Some insects hatch from eggs looking like tiny versions of the adults. They simply grow larger over time and shed their skins as they become too tight.

Some insects, like the dragonfly, have a three-phase lifecycle.

  • Egg→
  • Nymph→
  • Adult

A dragonfly spends most of its life as a nymph. It doesn’t look much like an adult dragonfly and lives underwater.

Some insects, like the butterfly, have a four-stage lifecycle (called complete metamorphosis).

  • Egg→
  • Larva→
  • Pupa→
  • Adult

Dragonfly Lifecycle: Egg, Nymph, Adult

Butterfly Lifecycle: Egg, Larva, Pupa, Adult

Butterfly Lifecycle Stages

The Egg Stage

Butterfly eggs are fixed to a leaf with a special glue which hardens rapidly. This is the same glue used by the pupa to attach its chrysalis to a plant. The egg stage normally only lasts a few weeks, although if they are laid close to winter, the eggs might rest until spring.

The Larva Stage

Butterfly larvae are called caterpillars – they eat, and eat, and EAT! They spend nearly all of their time either eating or looking for food. Caterpillars mature in a series of stages where they moult (shed their skin) as they get larger.

The Pupa Stage

When a larva is full-grown, it stops eating and begins wandering around looking for a good place to attach its chrysalis (usually the underside of a leaf).

The Adult Stage

After a few weeks (or in some cases months), an adult butterfly emerges from the chrysalis and hangs under its leaf. It then inflates its wings and waits for them to dry and harden. The drying process can take from 1 to 3 hours.

Butterfly wings are covered in scales. Most butterflies are very pretty to look at and can be very colorful. Adult butterflies don’t consume solid food – they drink liquids like nectar from flowers. They have long hollow tongues for that reason. Butterflies use their antennae to sense the air for wind and scents. They also see very well.

Some species of butterflies (like the monarch) live many months (nearly a year). They also migrate. Each year, millions of Monarch butterflies travel from Canada and the USA down to Mexico.

Fast Facts About Insects

Bees give us honey and help pollinate our crops and flowering plants

Without pollination, there wouldn’t be crops and flowers anymore

Ladybugs keep more harmful insects away from our gardens

Scientists study certain insects for clues on how to fight diseases

Earthworms move the earth around and help make it more fertile for our gardens

Ants can carry 5 to 20 times their body weight and work together in small or large groups to move heavier things.

Insect Crafts

Simple Little Ladybugs

  1. Take a Styrofoam ball and cut it in half. Each half will give us one ladybug.
  2. Using washable markers or paints, color the ladybug red with black spots (or whatever colors you like).
  3. Draw on the ladybug’s eyes and face.
  4. Use colored pipe cleaners and stick them in just behind the face to make the ladybug’s antennae.
  5. Voila! Have fun with your ladybugs!

Egg Carton Caterpillars

  1. Take an egg carton and cut out a strip of egg cups the size you want to make your caterpillar (at least 3). Be sure to leave all the egg cups in the strip attached to each other.
  2. Color the caterpillar as you like with washable markers or paints.
  3. Draw on the caterpillar’s face.
  4. Poke holes in the first cup right behind the face and attach pipe cleaners in the holes for the caterpillar’s antennae.
  5. Pull the pipe cleaners through the holes and make a knot underneath to stop them from slipping out.
