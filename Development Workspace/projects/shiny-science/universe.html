How the Universe Was Created

Woosh! The Big Bang

The Big Bang marks the moment when the universe began, around 13.8 billion years ago. This event wasn't an explosion in space, it was the expansion of space itself from an incredibly dense and hot state.

The Sun is the center of our solar system, and it is the closest star to Earth. Our solar system consists of the Sun and everything that travels around it, including planets, moons, asteroids, and comets.

The Sun provides the necessary energy and gravitational pull to keep our solar system intact. It is by far the largest object in the solar system, containing 99.86% of the solar system's mass.

How Long Will Our Sun Live?

Our Sun is currently about 4.6 billion years old and will continue to shine for at least another 7 billion years. While this seems like an incredibly long time, it's just a fraction of the Sun's overall lifecycle.

How Stars are Born

Stars are born in vast clouds of gas and dust, known as nebulae. Gravity pulls these clouds together until nuclear reactions begin, and a star is born. This process can take millions of years.

The Lifecycle of a Star

Stars have a lifecycle that can span billions of years, depending on their size.

Do Stars Live Forever?

Red Giants

Stars expand into red giants as they near the end of their lives, consuming their outer layers.

White Dwarfs

A red giant sheds its outer layers, leaving behind a dense, hot core known as a white dwarf.

Supernova

Larger stars explode in a supernova, releasing an enormous amount of energy and sometimes leaving behind a neutron star.

Hypernova

A hypernova is an even more energetic explosion that can result in a black hole. These explosions are responsible for gamma-ray bursts, which can be seen across the universe.

Black Holes

When the most massive stars collapse under their own gravity, they can form black holes, objects with such strong gravity that nothing can escape from them, not even light.

G A L A X I E S

What Are Galaxies?

Galaxies are vast collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound together by gravity. They come in various shapes and sizes, from spirals like our Milky Way to elliptical and irregular forms. Galaxies can contain billions to trillions of stars, and they are the building blocks of the universe, with each galaxy forming a unique cosmic system.

How Stars Form Galaxies

Stars form galaxies through the combined force of gravity. As stars are born in massive clouds of gas and dust (nebulae), their gravitational pull binds them together. Over time, these stars group into clusters, and gravity continues to attract more stars and matter, shaping them into galaxies. The process creates diverse structures, from spiral arms to elliptical formations, depending on their history and interactions.

What are the different types of galaxies?

Spiral Galaxies

Spiral galaxies have a flat, disk-like shape with winding spiral arms full of young stars, gas, and dust. The Milky Way is an example of a spiral galaxy.

Elliptical Galaxies

Elliptical galaxies are rounded or oval-shaped and contain mostly old stars. They have little gas and dust, meaning minimal new star formation occurs.

Irregular Galaxies

Irregular galaxies lack a distinct shape and often form due to gravitational interactions or collisions with other galaxies. They contain a mix of stars, gas, and dust.

Center of the Milky Way

What is the Closest Galaxy to Us?

The Andromeda Galaxy is the closest large galaxy to the Milky Way, located about 2.5 million light-years away. It is on a collision course with our galaxy and will merge with it in about 4 billion years.

What Holds Galaxies Together?

Galaxies are held together by gravity, which binds their stars, gas, and dark matter. Dark matter, an invisible substance making up about 80% of a galaxy's mass, plays a crucial role in stabilizing and maintaining their structure.

P L A N E T S

How Do Stars Form Planets?

Planets form from the leftover gas and dust in a star-forming region. After a star forms, the remaining material around it gathers into a rotating disk. Over time, this material clumps together through gravity and collisions, forming planets, moons, and other celestial objects.

What Types of Planets Exist?

Terrestrial Planets

Rocky worlds like Earth, Mars, Venus, and Mercury.

Gas Giants

Massive planets made mostly of gas, like Jupiter and Saturn.

Ice Giants

Planets like Uranus and Neptune, rich in ices and gases.

Dwarf Planets

Small, round bodies like Pluto that orbit the Sun but share their path with other objects.

What Holds Galaxies Together?

Earth is a terrestrial planet with a rocky surface, abundant water, and a breathable atmosphere. It is unique in supporting life due to its perfect distance from the Sun, a stable climate, and protective magnetic field.

Did You Know?

Jupiter is so massive that it doesn't technically orbit the Sun! Instead, both Jupiter and the Sun orbit a common point just above the Sun's surface, called the barycenter, due to Jupiter's immense gravitational pull.

What Are Moons?

Moons are natural satellites that orbit planets, dwarf planets, or even asteroids. They vary in size and composition, from small, irregularly shaped rocks to large, spherical bodies like Earth's Moon. Some moons, like Jupiter's Europa, may even harbor conditions suitable for life.
